Detecting Market Regimes Using Unsupervised Learning

1) Problem Statement

Financial markets do not behave the same way all the time. Periods of low volatility and steady growth (bull markets) are often followed by turbulent periods with sharp drawdowns and elevated risk (bear markets or crisis regimes). However, these regimes are not directly observed. They must be inferred from price and volatility behavior.

This project uses unsupervised learning to discover latent market regimes from historical price data, without any explicit labels.

Why This Problem Is Important

Identifying market regimes is valuable for several reasons:

  • Risk Management:
    Portfolio exposure can be adjusted when the market transitions into a high-volatility regime.

  • Asset Allocation & Timing:
    Regime-aware strategies often outperform static asset allocations, especially around crisis periods.

  • Interpretability:
    Regimes provide an intuitive narrative, such as calm growth, high-vol correction, or crisis regime, that is easier for decision-makers to understand than raw time-series data.

  • Unsupervised Learning Showcase:
    This project demonstrates how unsupervised models can extract structure from data when no target labels exist.

Project Goals

The objectives of this project are to:

  1. Define an unsupervised learning task using historical market data to identify latent regimes.
  2. Collect, clean, and engineer features such as daily returns, volatility, and drawdowns for a major market index.
  3. Fit and compare two unsupervised models:
    • A Hidden Markov Model (HMM)
    • A clustering model (e.g., K-Means)
  4. Evaluate models using unsupervised metrics and diagnostics, including:
    • Log-likelihood and BIC for the HMM
    • Silhouette score for clustering
    • Economic interpretability of regimes
  5. Interpret discovered regimes in terms of market behavior and financial intuition.
  6. Reflect on limitations of unsupervised regime detection and propose future improvements.

This project uses daily historical price data for a broad U.S. market index in order to detect latent market regimes. A broad index is ideal because it captures aggregate market behavior and reflects both calm and crisis periods.

For this analysis, we use one of the following:

  • S&P 500 Index (^GSPC), or
  • SPDR S&P 500 ETF (SPY) as a liquid tradable proxy

3) Data Cleaning & Feature Engineering

Before fitting any unsupervised learning model, the raw market price data must be cleaned and transformed into features that are informative for identifying latent market regimes.

The goal of this step is to construct variables that capture:

  • Short-term market movement
  • Medium-term trend behavior
  • Market risk and turbulence
  • Downside stress through drawdowns

These engineered features serve as the inputs for the Hidden Markov Model and clustering algorithms.

3.1 Data Cleaning

The following preprocessing steps are applied:

  1. The dataset is restricted to the adjusted price proxy (price).
  2. The time index is enforced as a proper DatetimeIndex.
  3. Any missing observations are removed.
  4. The data is sorted chronologically to preserve time ordering.

These steps ensure the time series is clean, continuous, and suitable for rolling window calculations.

  1. Daily Log Returns

5) Unsupervised Modeling Approach & Justification

The objective of this project is to identify latent market regimes without using any labeled outcome variable. Because regimes are unobserved, the modeling approach must rely entirely on patterns in the feature space and temporal structure of the data.

To ensure robustness and interpretability, two unsupervised approaches are employed:

  1. Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) to explicitly model regime persistence and transitions over time
  2. K-Means Clustering as a non-temporal baseline for grouping similar market conditions

Using both approaches allows us to compare:

  • A time-aware probabilistic model (HMM)
  • A static geometric clustering method (K-Means)

5.1 Why Hidden Markov Models?

Hidden Markov Models are well-suited for market regime detection because:

  • Market regimes are latent (unobserved) states
  • Market data are sequential and exhibit temporal dependence
  • Regimes tend to be persistent, switching infrequently

An HMM consists of:

  • A set of hidden states (regimes)
  • Transition probabilities between regimes
  • Emission distributions governing the observed features in each regime

In this project, a Gaussian HMM is used, where each regime emits a multivariate normal distribution over:

  • Log returns
  • Rolling volatility
  • Rolling trend
  • Drawdowns

This allows each regime to be characterized by its own mean return behavior and risk profile.

5.2 Why K-Means as a Baseline?

K-Means clustering is included as a complementary baseline because:

  • It is a widely used and interpretable distance-based unsupervised method
  • It groups observations based purely on feature similarity
  • It provides a way to validate whether regime-like groupings naturally emerge even without temporal structure

However, K-Means:

  • Does not model persistence
  • Does not account for time ordering
  • May produce noisy regime switching from day to day

Comparing K-Means results to HMM regimes helps assess whether time dynamics materially improve regime identification.

5.3 Feature Matrix for Modeling

Both models operate on the same standardized feature matrix:

  • log_return
  • roll_vol_21
  • roll_ret_63
  • drawdown_63

5.4 Selecting the Number of Regimes

Because the true number of market regimes is unknown, we evaluate multiple candidate values for the number of hidden states (K):

  • K = 2 (calm vs. stressed)
  • K = 3 (calm, transitional, crisis)
  • K = 4 (finer-grained market structure)

Model selection is based on:

  • Log-likelihood (model fit)
  • Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) (penalized model complexity)
  • Economic interpretability of the resulting regimes

For the K-Means baseline, the silhouette score is used as an unsupervised clustering quality metric.

These diagnostics guide the selection of an appropriate and parsimonious number of regimes.

5.5 Evaluation Philosophy for Unsupervised Models

Unlike supervised learning, unsupervised regime detection does not minimize prediction error against a known target. Instead, evaluation relies on a combination of:

  • Statistical fit metrics (log-likelihood, BIC)
  • Cluster separation metrics (silhouette score)
  • Temporal persistence of regimes
  • Economic interpretability, assessed using:
    • Average returns
    • Volatility levels
    • Drawdown severity
    • Regime durations and transition behavior

A regime model is considered successful if it produces:

  • Stable regimes over time
  • Distinct returnrisk profiles across states
  • Clear alignment with known periods of market stress

These criteria reflect the real-world use of regime models in portfolio construction and risk management.

6.1 Why a Time-Series Split is Required

Random traintest splits violate the temporal structure of financial data and can cause the model to:

  • Learn future volatility patterns that would not be observable in real time
  • Artificially inflate regime stability
  • Produce unrealistic transition dynamics

By splitting the dataset chronologically, we ensure:

  • All training information occurs strictly before the test period
  • Regime persistence and transitions reflect real-world deployment conditions

6.2 K-Means Model Selection Logic

K-Means clustering is evaluated using the silhouette score, which measures:

  • Within-cluster cohesion
  • Between-cluster separation

A silhouette score ranges from:

  • 1 poor clustering
  • 0 overlapping clusters
  • +1 strong separation

The optimal number of regimes for the K-Means baseline is selected as the value of K with the highest silhouette score, subject to economic interpretability.

6.3 Probabilistic Regime Model: Training Strategy

  • Standardized feature matrix
  • Chronological training window
  • Candidate regime counts K = {2, 3, 4}

  • Log-likelihood
  • Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC)
  • Regime interpretability

  • A true Hidden Markov Model (HMM) when available
  • A Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) as a robust probabilistic fallback

Both approaches produce:

  • Latent regime labels
  • Regime-specific returnrisk profiles
  • Information-criterion-based model selection

6.4 Probabilistic Model Selection

The Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) is used to select the optimal number of regimes for the probabilistic model.

BIC balances:

  • Model fit (log-likelihood)
  • Model complexity (number of parameters)

The preferred model is the one with the lowest BIC, provided that its regimes remain economically interpretable.

This prevents overfitting while preserving meaningful regime structure.

7.2 Regime Persistence and Stability

Two complementary measures of regime stability are examined:

  • Frequency of each regime how often each state appears
  • Average regime duration how long regimes persist before switching

Economically meaningful regime models typically produce:

  • One dominant low-volatility regime
  • One or more short but intense high-volatility regimes
  • Reasonable persistence (not daily oscillation)

Extremely short average durations would indicate unstable or noisy regime identification.

7.4 Comparing Probabilistic vs. K-Means Regimes

Key conceptual differences between the two approaches:

  • The probabilistic model:

    • Learns full multivariate distributions
    • Produces smooth, persistent regime sequences
    • Is better suited for economic interpretation

  • K-Means:

    • Uses geometric cluster separation only
    • Ignores time structure
    • Often produces more frequent switching

While both methods identify high- and low-volatility states, the probabilistic model typically provides more realistic and stable market regimes.

8) Conclusions, Limitations, and Future Extensions

This project demonstrated how unsupervised machine learning can be used to uncover latent market regimes from historical financial data without relying on labeled outcomes. Using only price-derived features such as returns, volatility, rolling trends, and drawdowns, both probabilistic regime models (Gaussian Mixture Models) and K-Means clustering were successfully applied to identify distinct market states.

The results show that financial markets are best understood as regime-switching systems rather than as a single stationary process. Calm, transitional, and crisis regimes exhibit sharply different returnrisk profiles, frequencies, and persistence patterns. In particular, high-volatility regimes account for a small fraction of total observations but contribute disproportionately to overall market drawdowns. This underscores the practical importance of regime-based modeling for risk management, portfolio allocation, and strategic investment decisions.

Compared to K-Means clustering, the probabilistic regime model produced more stable and economically interpretable regimes, with smoother transitions and clearer differentiation between market states. This highlights the value of incorporating distributional structure and probabilistic inference into unsupervised financial modeling.

Limitations

Despite these strengths, several important limitations should be noted:

  • The regimes are model-dependent and sensitive to:
    • Feature selection
    • Rolling window lengths
    • The assumed number of regimes
  • Gaussian Mixture Models assume normally distributed emissions, which may not fully capture the heavy tails and skewness present in financial returns.
  • Regime labels themselves are latent and unlabeled, so model validation relies on:
    • Indirect diagnostics (BIC, silhouette scores)
    • Economic interpretability
    • Visual alignment with known stress events
  • The analysis is based on a single market index proxy, which limits the ability to detect cross-asset or macroeconomic regime interactions.

Future Extensions and Improvements

Several natural extensions could significantly expand the scope and realism of this project:

  1. True Hidden Markov Models (HMMs)
    Incorporating explicit transition dynamics via HMMs would allow:

    • Direct estimation of regime persistence
    • Forward-looking regime probability forecasts

  2. Multi-Asset Regime Detection
    Extending the feature set to include:

    • Equity indices
    • Bond yields
    • Commodities or volatility indices
      would enable detection of broader macro-financial regimes.

  3. Regime-Aware Portfolio Strategies
    Using regime labels to dynamically adjust:

    • Equity exposure
    • Leverage
    • Hedging instruments
      would allow full backtesting of regime-based investment strategies.

  4. Non-Gaussian Emission Models
    Student-t mixtures or regime-switching GARCH models could better capture:

    • Fat-tailed returns
    • Volatility clustering
    • Asymmetric downside risk

  5. Online Regime Detection
    Implementing real-time filtering methods would enable:

    • Live regime classification
    • Adaptive risk control in production settings

Final Reflection

This project highlights one of the most powerful strengths of unsupervised learning: the ability to extract meaningful structure from complex systems without predefined labels. In contrast to supervised classification tasks (such as loan default prediction), regime detection reframes the problem from prediction to discovery. The primary output is not a binary decision but a deeper understanding of how market behavior evolves over time.

From a practical perspective, the ability to detect latent regimes provides a valuable foundation for:

  • Quantitative risk management
  • Macro-driven investment strategies
  • Financial stress testing
  • Economic state inference

Overall, this project demonstrates how unsupervised learning can bridge the gap between statistical modeling, financial theory, and real-world decision making.
